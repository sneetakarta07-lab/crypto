How to buy Bitcoin in Japan (step-by-step)

Beginners often think buying Bitcoin is complicated. In reality, it is usually a simple process if you choose a reputable exchange. The key is to do it step-by-step and focus on security. Below is a beginner-friendly flow that works for most Japanese users.

Step 2: Create your account

Sign up using an email address or mobile number. Use a strong password you have never used anywhere else. A password manager is helpful if you can use one.

Step 3: Complete KYC verification

In Japan, KYC is common on major exchanges. Verification helps exchanges follow anti-money laundering rules and protect users. If a platform says “no verification needed, unlimited trading,” treat it as a red flag.

Depending on your situation, you can deposit through supported methods or transfer crypto from another wallet/exchange. Beginners usually start with a small amount so they can learn without stress.

Search for a Bitcoin trading pair (for example BTC/USDT). Use a market order if you want a quick buy, or a limit order if you want to control the price. For beginners, limit orders can reduce emotional mistakes, but market orders are simpler.

Beginner rule: Don’t jump into futures trading on day one. Learn spot, learn wallet basics, then expand.

Crypto exchange fees in Japan (what to check)

Fees decide whether your trading is sustainable. Many beginners focus only on “trading fee,” but the real cost can also include withdrawal charges, conversion spreads, and network fees.

1) Spot trading fees

Spot fees are charged when you buy or sell at the standard market. These are typically a percentage of your trade. Lower fees matter more if you trade frequently.

2) Futures fees (only for experienced users)

Futures trading can amplify profits and losses. Fees can be low, but leverage can cause fast losses. If you are a beginner, skip futures until you can explain liquidation in simple words.

3) Withdrawal fees

Withdrawal fees depend on the coin and network used. Bitcoin withdrawals often cost more than some other coins. Always check withdrawal fees before you deposit large amounts.

4) Network fees

Network fees are paid to the blockchain network (not only the exchange). During busy periods, network fees can rise. This is normal in crypto and not always controlled by the exchange.

Hidden cost: spread

Some platforms show “zero fee” but earn money through a wider spread (difference between buy and sell price). That is why comparing “effective price” matters, not only the fee label.

Crypto tax in Japan (2026 basics)

Japan has a clear approach: profits from crypto activities can be taxable. The details can depend on your income level, your activity type, and your reporting method. If you trade often, accurate record-keeping becomes very important.

Common taxable events can include selling crypto for fiat, converting one crypto to another, or using crypto for purchases. If you plan to trade seriously in Japan, treat record-keeping like part of the job.

Safety tips for crypto users in Japan

Even in a regulated environment, personal security is still the #1 factor. Most losses come from phishing, weak passwords, fake apps, or falling for unrealistic promises.

  • Enable 2FA (authenticator app is better than SMS when available).
  • Use a unique password and never reuse it across sites.
  • Bookmark the real exchange URL to avoid phishing pages.
  • Start small until you understand deposits/withdrawals.
  • Consider a wallet for long-term holdings instead of leaving everything on exchanges.
  • Avoid hype: if someone guarantees returns, it’s usually a scam.
