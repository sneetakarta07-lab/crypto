Taxes are not the “fun” part of crypto, but in Japan they matter. Many beginners focus only on buying Bitcoin, then ignore taxes until they need withdrawals, reporting, or they have multiple trades across months. This page explains the topic in simple language so you can build good habits early.

Why tax planning matters for crypto in Japan

When you trade, swap coins, or realize profit, it can create a taxable event depending on how your country treats crypto. The biggest mistake is not tracking your trades and fees. Even if you use one exchange, you still need clear records.

Common transactions that may be taxable

  • Selling BTC/ETH for fiat or stablecoins
  • Swapping one coin for another (crypto-to-crypto)
  • Spending crypto on goods/services
  • Some rewards/earnings (depending on rules)

What records you should keep (beginner checklist)

  • Date/time of each trade
  • Buy price and sell price
  • Trade fees and withdrawal fees
  • Deposits/withdrawals (TXIDs if available)
  • Notes: why you traded (helps later)

How to track your trades easily

The easiest way is to export your exchange history monthly and store it safely. If you use multiple exchanges, keep separate exports, then merge them in one place (sheet or tracking tool). Even simple record-keeping is better than nothing.

Simple examples (beginner)

Example 1: You buy BTC, then sell later for profit. That profit may be taxable depending on rules.

Example 2: You swap ETH to BTC. Even without fiat, the swap can be treated as a taxable event in many systems.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Not saving trade history before it expires
  • Ignoring fees (fees affect your net profit)
  • Using many wallets/exchanges without tracking
  • Assuming “small trades don’t matter”
